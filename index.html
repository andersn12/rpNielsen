Optimal Electrolysis Voltage for the Rate of Hydrogen Generation From Seawater

Historical Background


  • Energy reliance on fossil fuels and other environmentally-harmful energy sources has motivated research on alternatives to fossil fuels
  • Seawater electrolysis uses seawater instead of freshwater, using the salt as a catalyst in the chemical reaction separating the hydrogen and oxygen from water
  • While salt is a catalyst for electrolysis, it also causes corrosion of the anode, where oxygen is produced, which reduces the efficiency of the reaction

Rationale

  • To make seawater electrolysis a viable alternative to the burning of fossil fuels, the reaction must take place at a very large scale, be as efficient as possible, and be as cost-effective as possible
  • An observation was made that higher electrolysis voltages cause higher hydrogen production in seawater
  • It was hypothesized that an optimal electrolysis voltage exists to efficiently produce hydrogen at the fastest rate possible
  • It was predicted that the rate of hydrogen generation would decline after a certain amount of time due to anode corrosion, which would depend on the electrolysis voltage

Abstract

Large-scale electrolysis for hydrogen production can potentially provide a clean alternative to environmentally hazardous energy sources that are used today. Besides creating clean energy, seawater electrolysis also purifies seawater into drinking water. The study analyzed how various electrolysis voltages impacted the rate of the hydrogen generation from seawater. It was hypothesized that an optimal electrolysis voltage exists to efficiently produce hydrogen at the fastest rate possible. It was predicted that the rate of hydrogen would lessen after a certain amount of time due to corrosion of the anode. During the research, electrolysis was carried out on seven different voltages, two of them being controls, with three trials for each condition. Each trial was run for 24 hours and the total volume of hydrogen was recorded every 30 minutes. The study did not conclude that a certain voltage was optimal for hydrogen generation. However, the results showed an unexpected difference between the rate of hydrogen generation for the 4.0 volt condition compared to the 5.0 volt condition. In addition to this, it was concluded that corrosion influenced the results of the study, which could motivate future studies to research corrosion during seawater electrolysis.
